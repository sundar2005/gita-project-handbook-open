Week 6 · Chapter 6 — Dhyāna Yoga: The Yoga of Meditation and Self-Mastery

Student Theme — Training the Mind Without Fighting Yourself

Chapter Summary

What Happens in Chapter Six

Chapter Six develops the discipline of meditation as the natural continuation of selfless action. Lord Shri Krishna explains that true yoga is achieved by mastering the mind rather than abandoning responsibility. He describes the qualities of a genuine yogi, the proper practice of meditation, moderation in daily living, and the importance of perseverance. Arjuna expresses concern about the restless nature of the mind, and Lord Shri Krishna assures him that through practice (abhyāsa) and detachment (vairāgya) the mind can be brought under control. The chapter concludes by declaring that the highest yogi is the one who, with faith and devotion, keeps the mind steadily fixed upon the Lord.

Voices of the Tradition

How the great teachers illuminate this chapter — each from their own lineage of wisdom.

Ādi ŚaṅkarācāryaAdvaita Vedānta

Explains that meditation prepares the purified mind for steady knowledge of the Self. The restless mind reflects lingering attachment and ignorance. Through disciplined practice, sense control, and discrimination, the seeker becomes established in contemplation of the true Self. Meditation is not an end in itself but a means of abiding in Self-knowledge and inner freedom.

RāmānujācāryaViśiṣṭādvaita

Teaches that meditation reaches its highest fulfillment when the mind lovingly contemplates the Supreme Lord. Devotion, remembrance, and surrender deepen concentration and strengthen the relationship between the individual soul and God. A disciplined life supports unwavering devotion, allowing meditation to become an expression of loving communion.

MadhvācāryaDvaita

Emphasizes that the individual soul depends upon the grace of the Supreme Lord even while practicing meditation. Self-discipline is essential, yet success ultimately comes through Divine guidance. Meditation strengthens awareness of the Lord's presence and inspires the seeker to perform every duty with humility and steadfast faith.

Swami ChinmayanandaContemporary teacher

Presents meditation as a practical discipline for developing emotional balance, concentration, and purposeful living. He stresses moderation in food, sleep, work, and recreation, explaining that a balanced lifestyle supports a balanced mind. Meditation enables individuals to respond thoughtfully instead of reacting impulsively, making it an essential life skill.

Swami MukundanandaContemporary teacher

Highlights the psychological dimension of meditation. The mind naturally wanders, but it should be gently and repeatedly brought back to its higher focus without discouragement. Progress comes through patience, consistency, and self-compassion. Faith in the Lord strengthens perseverance and transforms meditation into a joyful journey rather than a burden.

Common Threads of Dharma All five teachers affirm that mastery of the mind is essential for living according to Dharma. Meditation requires discipline, perseverance, humility, and guidance. A balanced lifestyle, steady practice, freedom from selfish attachment, and devotion to the Supreme Lord gradually cultivate inner peace and wisdom. The disciplined mind becomes an instrument for righteous action.

Every life has moments where…

…we realize that the greatest challenge is not the world around us, but the thoughts within us.

There are days when everything seems to be going well, yet our minds remain restless. We replay conversations. We worry about tomorrow. We compare ourselves with others. We imagine problems that have not yet happened. Our minds can become our greatest friend. They can also become our greatest obstacle. Chapter Six teaches us that one of life's greatest achievements is learning to understand and master our own minds.

One question — When your mind will not settle, is the answer to force it quiet, or to learn to work with it patiently?

A moment you may know — You sit down to focus, fully intending to concentrate, and within minutes your attention has drifted somewhere else entirely.

This chapter's promise — You will learn that the mind can be trained the way any skill is trained: not by fighting yourself, but through steady, patient practice.

Today's Kurukshetra

The battlefield turns inward

Arjuna has begun to understand Dharma. He has learned about wisdom. He has learned about selfless action. He has discovered that peace comes from living according to Dharma rather than chasing temporary rewards. Yet Lord Shri Krishna now turns the conversation inward.

Knowing what is right is one challenge. Living it consistently is another. Why? Because the mind is constantly moving. It is influenced by desires, distractions, memories, fears, habits, and emotions. If the mind is left undisciplined, even good intentions become difficult to sustain. Lord Shri Krishna therefore introduces a lifelong practice — not merely meditation, but self-mastery.

Understanding the Situation

Imagine trying to study for an important examination. You sit down with every intention of concentrating. Within a few minutes your mind begins to wander. You remember a message on your phone. You wonder what your friends are doing. You begin thinking about the weekend. A notification appears. Suddenly thirty minutes have passed and nothing has been accomplished. Has this ever happened to you? The challenge was never the textbook. The challenge was the wandering mind.

Now imagine the same thing happening with kindness. With honesty. With patience. With self-control. Many times we know exactly what the right choice is, yet our minds drift toward comfort, distraction, fear, or impulse. Lord Shri Krishna teaches that learning to guide the mind is one of the greatest disciplines in life.

The Court of Dharma™

Six steps into self-mastery

One Situation
Lord Shri Krishna teaches Arjuna that lasting peace requires mastery over the mind.
One Dilemma
How can we live according to Dharma when our thoughts and emotions constantly pull us in different directions?
One Question
How can I become the master of my mind instead of allowing my mind to master me?
One Conversation
Lord Shri Krishna explains that the mind can become either our greatest friend or our greatest enemy. When guided with discipline, wisdom, and regular practice, it supports growth, compassion, courage, and inner peace; when left uncontrolled, it becomes restless, distracted, and driven by temporary desires. Arjuna honestly admits that controlling the mind seems as difficult as controlling the wind. Lord Shri Krishna agrees the mind is restless — but assures him that through practice (abhyāsa) and detachment (vairāgya) it can gradually become steady. He does not expect perfection; He teaches progress through patient, consistent effort.
One Virtue
Self-Discipline — learning to choose what is right over what is merely easy, and to direct our attention instead of letting distractions direct us.
One Life Practice
Choose one small daily habit that strengthens your self-discipline, and keep it for seven days. (Full practice below.)
Krishna's Teaching · Listen with Arjuna

Two verses on the mind

Verse 1 — Be your own friend · 6.5

उद्धरेदात्मनात्मानं नात्मानमवसादयेत् ।
आत्मैव ह्यात्मनो बन्धुरात्मैव रिपुरात्मन: ॥

uddhared ātmanātmānaṁ nātmānam avasādayet
ātmaiva hyātmano bandhur ātmaiva ripur ātmanaḥ

“Let him raise the self by the Self and not let the self become depressed; for verily is the Self the friend of the self, and also the Self the self's enemy;”

— Bhagavad Gita 6.5 · trans. Annie Besant (1922), public domain — via Wikisource

Words to Remember — uddharet lift up, elevate · ātmā the self / the mind · bandhu friend · ripu enemy.

In plain words. The same mind that can lift you up can also drag you down — and the difference is how you train it. No one else can do this work for you. Your mind is not your enemy by nature; it becomes a friend when you learn to guide it with patience.

Reflect: In the last week, was your mind more of a friend to you or more of an obstacle?

Verse 2 — Restless, yet trainable · 6.35

असंशयं महाबाहो मनो दुर्निग्रहं चलम् ।
अभ्यासेन तु कौन्तेय वैराग्येण च गृह्यते ॥

asanśhayaṁ mahā-bāho mano durnigrahaṁ chalam
abhyāsena tu kaunteya vairāgyeṇa cha gṛihyate

“Without doubt, O mighty-armed, the mind is hard to curb and restless; but it may be curbed by constant practice and by dispassion.”

— Bhagavad Gita 6.35 · trans. Annie Besant (1922), public domain — via Wikisource

Read this carefully — “control” does not mean force

Krishna does not tell Arjuna to fight or punish his own mind. He offers two gentle tools: abhyāsa (steady, repeated practice) and vairāgya (loosening our grip on cravings and distractions). Training the mind is like taming a restless animal with patience, not with a whip. If your attention wanders a hundred times, success is simply bringing it back a hundred and one.

Words to Remember — chala restless, moving · durnigraha hard to restrain · abhyāsa practice · vairāgya detachment.

In plain words. Notice how honest this verse is. Krishna does not deny that the mind is hard to steady — He agrees with Arjuna. Then He gives real hope: difficult is not the same as impossible. Two ordinary tools, used daily, are enough.

Reflect: What is one small practice you could return to daily, even when you don't feel like it?

Chapter Six presents meditation not as an escape from life, but as a way of cultivating clarity, balance, and inner strength. A disciplined mind helps us understand Dharma more clearly, and lets us respond thoughtfully rather than react impulsively. Krishna also gives great encouragement: even sincere effort that seems incomplete is never wasted. Every step taken toward wisdom strengthens the journey. Spiritual growth is not measured by speed, but by sincerity and perseverance.

Dharma Lens

When self-discipline and self-kindness seem to pull apart

Chapter Six honors steady effort and patient self-acceptance — which can quietly collide in real life.

A situation without an obvious answer

Maya promised herself she would practise piano for thirty minutes every evening this month — a small vow to build discipline. Two weeks in, she is exhausted from exams, grieving a friendship that ended, and most nights she manages only ten minutes before her mind gives out. One voice says: keep the promise no matter what, or the discipline means nothing. Another voice says: forcing yourself while running on empty is its own kind of harm. Is honoring self-discipline about pushing through — or is the disciplined choice sometimes to rest without guilt?

The Gita praises steady practice (abhyāsa) and warns against degrading the self (6.5). So which is Dharma here — the full thirty minutes, or the honest ten?

The Gita's own language suggests this is not a simple either/or. Verse 6.5 says to elevate the self, not to crush it; verse 6.35 offers practice and detachment together. Real self-discipline is not the same as self-punishment. Sometimes it looks like showing up even when tired; sometimes it looks like resting wisely so you can return tomorrow. A thoughtful person might weigh Maya's night in more than one defensible way — the deciding question is whether the choice comes from genuine care for her growth, or from either harshness or mere avoidance.

Character in Practice · Self-Discipline

What it means. Learning to choose what is right over what is merely easy, and to direct your attention instead of letting distractions direct you.

What it does not mean. Punishing yourself, or becoming rigid and joyless. Every small act of discipline is meant to strengthen your freedom, not shrink it.

In daily life. Beginning homework before checking your phone; keeping a small promise to yourself for a week; speaking kindly even when irritated.

How it's misread. As harshness or grim willpower. Real self-discipline is patient — it returns after a lapse instead of quitting in disgust.

Recognizing progress — without self-righteousness. You notice you can return to a good habit after breaking it, without a spiral of guilt. Pair discipline with self-compassion — the two grow together.

Student-Life Connection

The revision session that dissolves the moment a notification lights up your screen. The training run you skip because motivation faded on day four. The temper you lose with a sibling when you are tired. Chapter Six gently suggests: none of these is a verdict on who you are. Each is simply the restless mind at work — and the mind is trainable. Discipline is not one heroic effort; it is the quiet decision to return.

Leadership Lab

Imagine you have decided to improve in something important — your studies, your health, your relationships, your music, your sport. You begin with enthusiasm. After a few days, motivation fades, distractions return, and you feel like giving up. What would Lord Shri Krishna encourage?

He would not ask whether you succeeded perfectly. He would ask whether you returned to the practice. Great leaders are not those who never struggle. They are those who return, again and again, to what is right. Consistency often matters more than intensity.

Weekly Activity · Seven Days, One Habit

Purpose: experience self-discipline as steady practice rather than force, and observe your own mind honestly. Time: 15–20 min setup, then a few minutes daily for a week · Format: individual, shared back in class · Materials: journal, a simple tracker (paper or phone).

  1. Choose one small habit to strengthen your self-discipline — for example: beginning homework before checking your phone; five minutes of quiet reflection each morning; reading one verse of the Gita a day; practising gratitude before sleep; or speaking kindly when irritated.
  2. Write the habit down and keep it small enough to do even on a bad day.
  3. Do it each day for seven days. Aim for consistency, not perfection.
  4. If you miss a day, do not quit — simply return the next day. Returning is the practice.
  5. At the end of the week, write in your journal: What challenged me? What became easier? What did I learn about my own mind?

Teacher Note — Chapter 6

Objective: students can explain that the mind can be either friend or enemy (6.5) and can name practice (abhyāsa) and detachment (vairāgya) as the Gita's gentle, patient tools for training it (6.35).

Sensitive-topic alert: meditation and “self-mastery” can be heard by anxious or perfectionist students as pressure to control every thought. Frame discipline as patient return, never as force or self-punishment; some students may raise stress, sleep, or low mood — respond with care and refer on where needed.

Likely question: “What if I try and keep failing?” → Use 6.35: even Krishna calls the mind hard to restrain. Success is returning to the practice, not never slipping.

Misconception to avoid: equating self-discipline with harshness or joylessness. Distinguish elevating the self (6.5) from crushing it.

Transition to Chapter 7: now that Arjuna has begun to master the mind, Krishna invites him to discover the source of all wisdom and existence — the journey moves from self-understanding toward understanding the Divine.

Looking Ahead

In Chapter Seven, Lord Shri Krishna begins revealing a deeper understanding of Himself. Arjuna has learned how to act. He has learned how to think. He has begun learning how to master the mind. Now Lord Shri Krishna invites him to discover the source of all wisdom, all existence, and all Dharma. The journey now moves from self-understanding toward understanding the Divine.

A gentle note on well-being — training the mind takes patience, and some days are harder than others. If restlessness, worry, or low mood ever feels heavy to carry alone, that is not a failure of discipline. Please talk with a parent, teacher, or another trusted adult. Asking for support is itself a strong and wise choice.
