Week 10 · Chapter 10 — Vibhūti Yoga: The Yoga of Divine Glories

Student Theme — Learning to See Greatness Without Envy

Every life has moments where…

…we begin to see greatness not as something to admire from a distance, but as a reminder of the Divine presence woven throughout creation.

There are moments that leave us speechless. Watching the sunrise over the ocean. Standing beneath a sky filled with stars. Listening to music that touches the heart. Meeting someone whose kindness changes another person's life. Witnessing courage in the face of adversity. Experiencing the unconditional love of a parent. In those moments, we often sense that life is greater than ourselves.

Chapter Ten invites us to discover why. Lord Shri Krishna teaches Arjuna to recognize the Divine reflected in everything that is noble, beautiful, powerful, wise, and inspiring.

One question — When you notice someone who is more talented, admired, or gifted than you, does it stir gratitude or quiet envy?

A moment you may know — A classmate wins the prize you hoped for, and you have to choose between resenting their gift and celebrating it.

This chapter's promise — You will learn to see greatness in others and in the world as a reflection of something Divine — a reason for wonder and gratitude rather than comparison.

Today's Kurukshetra

How can I remember the Divine all day long?

Arjuna's understanding has deepened. He no longer questions whether Lord Shri Krishna is merely a wise teacher. He now recognizes Him as the Supreme Divine. Yet Arjuna desires to know something more. How can he remember the Divine throughout the day? How can he remain connected to Lord Shri Krishna while living an active life?

Lord Shri Krishna responds by revealing His Vibhūtis — His divine manifestations and glories that can be recognized throughout creation. He does not ask Arjuna to escape the world. He teaches Arjuna how to see the Divine within it.

Understanding the Situation

Imagine visiting an art museum. You admire a beautiful painting. Without meeting the artist, you begin to understand something about the one who created it. The beauty of the artwork reflects the creativity of the artist.

Now imagine looking at the world in the same way. The order of nature. The rhythm of the seasons. The brilliance of the human mind. Acts of compassion. Moments of extraordinary courage. The majesty of mountains. The vastness of the oceans. The wonder of life itself. Lord Shri Krishna teaches that wherever we encounter excellence, wisdom, beauty, strength, compassion, or greatness, we are witnessing a reflection of the Divine. These glimpses remind us of the One from whom all greatness originates.

One Conversation
Lord Shri Krishna lovingly describes many examples of His divine manifestations. Among mountains, He is the Himalayas. Among rivers, He is the Ganga. Among lights, He is the radiant sun. Among warriors, He is strength guided by Dharma. Among leaders, He is wisdom. Among words, He is truth. These examples are not meant to limit the Divine; they are meant to train Arjuna's vision. Whenever Arjuna encounters greatness rooted in Dharma, he is invited to remember its Divine source — and to see every moment as an opportunity for gratitude, reverence, and wonder.

Krishna's Teaching · Listen with Arjuna

Two verses on seeing the Divine in all things

Verse 1 — Seated in every heart · 10.20

अहमात्मा गुडाकेश सर्वभूताशयस्थित: ।
अहमादिश्च मध्यं च भूतानामन्त एव च ॥

aham ātmā guḍākeśha sarva-bhūtāśhaya-sthitaḥ
aham ādiśh cha madhyaṁ cha bhūtānām anta eva cha

“I, O Gudakesha, am the Self, seated in the heart of all beings; I am the beginning, the middle, and also the end of all beings.”

— Bhagavad Gita 10.20 · trans. Annie Besant (1922), public domain — via Wikisource

Words to Remember — ātmā the self, soul · sarva-bhūta all living beings · āśhaya-sthitaḥ seated within · ādi-madhya-anta beginning, middle, and end.

In plain words. Before Krishna lists any glory, He says something quieter and deeper: the Divine is already seated in the heart of every living being. The greatness we notice “out there” is never truly far away — it is a reflection of the same presence that dwells within us and within everyone we meet.

Reflect: If the Divine dwells in every heart, how might that change the way you look at someone you envy?

Verse 2 — A spark of My splendor · 10.41

यद्यद्विभूतिमत्सत्त्वं श्रीमदूर्जितमेव वा ।
तत्तदेवावगच्छ त्वं मम तेजोंऽशसम्भवम् ॥

yad yad vibhūtimat sattvaṁ śhrīmad ūrjitam eva vā
tat tad evāvagachchha tvaṁ mama tejo 'nśha-sambhavam

“Whatsoever is glorious, good, beautiful, and mighty, understand thou that to go forth from a fragment of My splendour.”

— Bhagavad Gita 10.41 · trans. Annie Besant (1922), public domain — via Wikisource

What this does not mean

This verse is not saying that talented or successful people are more Divine than others, or that we should worship human greatness. It says the opposite: every beauty and strength we admire is only a spark — a small reflection of a far greater source. That is why greatness in others is a reason for gratitude and wonder, never for envy or for putting anyone (including ourselves) on a pedestal.

Words to Remember — vibhūti glory, manifestation · śhrīmat beautiful, glorious · ūrjitam powerful · tejo 'nśha a fragment of splendor.

In plain words. When you meet real beauty, brilliance, or strength, you are seeing a small spark of something infinitely greater. This is why we can appreciate greatness without becoming attached to it — the greatness belongs ultimately to its Divine source. Recognizing this cultivates both gratitude and humility.

Reflect: What is one glorious thing — in nature, art, or a person — that lately filled you with wonder?

Chapter Ten teaches that the Divine is not distant from creation. These examples are not intended to create comparison; they are invitations to awareness. Every noble quality reflects something of the Divine, and the Bhagavad Gita therefore encourages us to appreciate greatness without becoming attached to it.

Dharma Lens

When someone else's light feels like your shadow

Chapter Ten asks us to celebrate greatness in others as a spark of the Divine. But what happens when that greatness belongs to someone whose success costs us something?

A situation without an obvious answer

Meera and her closest friend both auditioned for the one lead role in the school play. Her friend got it — and deserved it. Everyone can see the friend is genuinely gifted. Meera wants to be happy for her, and part of her truly is. But another part aches, because she worked just as hard and now feels invisible. When her friend asks her to help run lines every evening, Meera has to decide: does she throw herself into supporting a talent she quietly wishes were her own — or is it more honest to step back and protect her own heart for a while?

Is gratitude here about celebrating her friend's spark of splendor wholeheartedly — or is forcing a smile she doesn't yet feel its own kind of dishonesty?

The Gita points toward seeing a friend's gift as a reflection of the Divine rather than a verdict on our own worth — yet it never asks us to fake a feeling we haven't grown into. A thoughtful person might handle Meera's moment in more than one way. She might choose to help and let genuine gladness slowly replace the ache; she might be honest about needing a little distance first; she might do both. What matters is whether her choice moves her toward gratitude and away from resentment — even if it takes time to get there.

Character in Practice · Gratitude

What it means. Gratitude begins with noticing — the habit of recognizing the gifts that often go unnoticed: the people who support us, the opportunities we receive, the beauty of creation, the ability to learn, the privilege of serving others.

What it does not mean. Ignoring life's challenges or pretending everything is fine. Gratitude reminds us that even during difficult times, reasons for hope remain.

In daily life. Thanking a teacher for a correction; noticing a meal, a friend, or a quiet moment; celebrating someone else's success instead of comparing yourself to it.

How it's misread. As weakness, or as owing something to everyone. Real gratitude is not obligation — it is the freedom of a heart less focused on what is missing.

Recognizing progress — without self-righteousness. You find yourself noticing blessings that were always present. Pair gratitude with humility — remembering that every gift carries the responsibility to serve others.

Student-Life Connection

The classmate whose grades you scroll past with a knot in your stomach. The teammate who makes it look effortless. The sibling everyone praises. Chapter Ten gently reframes them: each is carrying a spark of something greater than themselves — and so are you. Envy shrinks the world to a scoreboard; gratitude opens it back up, so that another person's light no longer dims your own.

Leadership Lab

Imagine two students receiving an award. One becomes proud and believes success belongs entirely to them. The other feels thankful — they recognize the encouragement of parents, the guidance of teachers, the support of friends, the opportunities they were given. Which student is more likely to continue growing?

Lord Shri Krishna reminds us that genuine greatness never separates us from others; it deepens our humility. True leaders celebrate success with gratitude rather than pride. They understand that every gift carries the responsibility to serve others.

Weekly Activity · The Gratitude Journal

Purpose: train the mind to recognize blessings that were always present, and to see greatness without envy. Time: about 10 minutes each evening across the week · Format: individual, optional sharing in class · Materials: a notebook or journal, a pen.

  1. Each evening this week, write down three things for which you are genuinely grateful.
  2. Do not repeat the same answers each day — look carefully. It might be a conversation with a friend, a lesson learned from a mistake, a teacher's encouragement, a family meal, or a quiet moment of prayer.
  3. Once during the week, let one of your three be a good quality you admire in another person — and thank the Divine source behind it instead of comparing.
  4. At the end of the week, read your list back and write one sentence about how your perspective changed.
Try It This Week

The Spark-Spotting Practice

Once a day, pause when you notice something excellent — a kindness, a skill, a piece of beauty — and silently say to yourself, “a spark of something greater.” Let admiration replace comparison, even for a moment.

Notice how differently the day feels when greatness becomes a reason for wonder rather than a measure of where you fall short.

Reflection & Journal Page

1. Personal. Think about someone you admire deeply. What qualities make that person inspiring — kindness, integrity, courage, wisdom, generosity?

2. Dharma. What do those qualities reveal about the Divine — and how might you cultivate one of them in your own life?

3. Character. If Lord Shri Krishna were walking beside you today, what signs of His presence might He encourage you to notice that you usually overlook?

4. Action commitment. One person whose greatness I will celebrate instead of envy this week — and how:

5. A private question (you never have to share this): Whose success has quietly stung you lately — and what would it take for you to feel genuine gladness for them?

A note on well-being. If comparing yourself to others often leaves you feeling small, worthless, or hopeless — not just briefly disappointed — that is worth taking seriously and not carrying alone. Gratitude is a practice, not a cure, and it is not a way of silencing real pain. Please talk with a parent, teacher, school counselor, or another trusted adult. Reaching out is an act of strength, and you deserve support.

Ask at Home

For a parent or guardian — please listen more than you speak. This is a conversation, not a quiz.

1. What is something in your life that you feel deeply grateful for, that you might not often say out loud?

2. Was there a time you had to be happy for someone else's success while wishing it were yours? How did you handle it?

3. Where do you most often notice something greater than yourself — in nature, in people, in prayer, somewhere else?

Teacher Note — Chapter 10

Objective: students can explain that the Gita presents all greatness as a reflection of the Divine (Vibhūti), and can describe gratitude as the response that lets us admire greatness without envy.

Sensitive-topic alert: comparison and envy touch real self-worth. Some students silently measure themselves against peers daily. Keep the tone warm; use the well-being note, and avoid turning sharing into another competition.

Likely question: “If all greatness is God's, do people still deserve credit for effort?” → Effort and gift are both real; recognizing the Divine source adds gratitude, it doesn't erase responsibility or hard work.

Misconception to avoid: that 10.41 ranks people by talent, or that gratitude means faking happiness. Distinguish genuine, growing gladness from forced smiles.
