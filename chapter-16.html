Week 16 · Chapter 16 — Daivāsura Sampad Vibhāga Yoga: The Yoga of Divine and Destructive Tendencies

Student Theme — The Qualities We Strengthen Become Who We Are

Chapter Summary

What Happens in Chapter Sixteen

Chapter Sixteen contrasts the qualities that elevate human character with those that lead to suffering and spiritual decline. Lord Shri Krishna describes the Daivī Sampad (divine qualities), such as fearlessness, purity, self-control, compassion, truthfulness, humility, and steadfastness in Dharma. He then explains the Āsurī Sampad (demoniac qualities), including pride, hypocrisy, anger, greed, arrogance, and disregard for righteousness. The chapter teaches that these qualities shape our choices, relationships, and destiny. Lord Shri Krishna concludes by directing Arjuna to rely upon the guidance of the scriptures in determining right conduct and living according to Dharma.

Voices of the Tradition

How the great teachers illuminate this chapter — each from their own lineage of wisdom.

Ādi ŚaṅkarācāryaAdvaita Vedānta

Explains that divine qualities purify the mind and prepare it for Self-knowledge, while demoniac qualities strengthen ignorance and attachment. Freedom is attained by cultivating virtues that support discrimination, self-mastery, and detachment from ego. The scriptures provide the reliable means for understanding righteous conduct.

RāmānujācāryaViśiṣṭādvaita

Teaches that divine qualities naturally arise in the heart of one who loves and serves the Supreme Lord. Humility, compassion, truthfulness, and self-control are outward expressions of devotion. Pride and selfishness distance the individual from God, whereas surrender and Dharma draw the devotee closer to Him.

MadhvācāryaDvaita

Emphasizes that the distinction between divine and demoniac qualities reflects one's response to the Supreme Lord. The seeker should cultivate virtues through faithful living, righteous conduct, and dependence upon Divine grace. The authority of scripture protects individuals from self-deception and guides them toward lasting spiritual progress.

Swami ChinmayanandaContemporary teacher

Presents this chapter as a practical guide to character development. Every day, individuals choose attitudes and habits that either strengthen noble qualities or reinforce harmful tendencies. Self-awareness, discipline, and conscious effort enable us to replace destructive habits with virtues that benefit both ourselves and society.

Swami MukundanandaContemporary teacher

Highlights that every person has the capacity to grow. Negative tendencies need not define us. Through prayer, self-reflection, uplifting company, devotion, and disciplined practice, divine qualities become stronger while selfish tendencies gradually lose their influence. Character is shaped by the choices we repeatedly make.

Common Threads of Dharma All five teachers affirm that Dharma is expressed through character. Divine qualities reflect alignment with truth, humility, compassion, and self-control, while demoniac qualities arise from ego, selfishness, and ignorance. The scriptures serve as the enduring guide for distinguishing right from wrong and cultivating virtues that lead to personal and spiritual growth.

Every life has moments where…

…our character is revealed by the choices we make when no one else is watching.

There are moments in life when no teacher is present. No parent is watching. No friend knows what decision we will make. No reward is promised. No punishment is guaranteed. Only one question remains: “What kind of person do I choose to be?” Character is not built in those moments. It is revealed. Every choice strengthens something within us, and every decision moves us closer to the person we are becoming. Chapter Sixteen teaches that the greatest battle in life is not fought against other people. It is fought within ourselves.

One question — When no one would ever know, which version of yourself shows up?

A moment you may know — You find a mistake in your favour that no one else noticed, and part of you wants to quietly let it stand.

This chapter's promise — You will discover that character is not a fixed label but a garden — and that the qualities you water each day are the ones that grow.

Today's Kurukshetra

Two paths, chosen every day

Lord Shri Krishna has guided Arjuna from confusion to understanding. He has revealed Dharma. He has explained Karma Yoga. He has taught self-mastery. He has revealed His Divine nature and shown Arjuna the Universal Form. He has taught devotion, and explained the eternal Self and the Three Guṇas.

Now Lord Shri Krishna describes two very different sets of tendencies that every human being may choose to strengthen. One set nourishes the qualities that help us live according to Dharma. The other feeds qualities that gradually separate us from wisdom, peace, and truth. The choice belongs to each one of us — every day.

Understanding the Situation

Imagine two gardens growing side by side. Both receive sunlight. Both receive rain. Yet one garden is carefully tended: weeds are removed, healthy plants are nourished, and it flourishes. The other garden is ignored: weeds spread, the healthiest plants struggle, and eventually the weeds take over.

Our character develops in much the same way. Kindness grows when it is practised. Honesty grows when it is chosen. Patience grows when it is exercised. But so do pride, anger, greed, dishonesty, and selfishness. Whatever we repeatedly nourish becomes stronger. Lord Shri Krishna invites Arjuna to become a careful gardener of his own character.

The Court of Dharma™

Six steps into the tendencies we cultivate

One Situation
Lord Shri Krishna describes the tendencies that lead toward inner freedom and the tendencies that lead away from Dharma.
One Dilemma
Which qualities should I intentionally strengthen, and which should I gradually let go?
One Question
What kind of character am I cultivating through my daily choices?
One Conversation
Lord Shri Krishna first describes the qualities of a life rooted in Dharma — fearlessness, purity of heart, self-discipline, compassion, truthfulness, patience, humility, forgiveness, generosity, gentleness, freedom from anger, and steadiness. He then contrasts them with tendencies that gradually lead away from wisdom — pride, arrogance, selfishness, harshness, dishonesty, anger, greed, and lack of self-control. These often promise temporary satisfaction, yet quietly weaken character and disturb inner peace. He is not asking Arjuna to judge others; He is inviting him to honestly examine his own heart.
One Virtue
Integrity — living by the same values whether others are watching or not: the quiet consistency between what we believe, what we say, and what we do. Integrity is not perfection; it is faithfulness to Dharma, and the honesty to correct our own course.
One Life Practice
Choose one Divine quality to practise intentionally every day, and review it each night. (Full practice below.)
Krishna's Teaching · Listen with Arjuna

Two verses on the qualities we strengthen

Verse 1 — The saintly virtues · 16.1–3

अभयं सत्त्वसंशुद्धिर्ज्ञानयोगव्यवस्थिति: ।
दानं दमश्च यज्ञश्च स्वाध्यायस्तप आर्जवम् ॥

abhayaṁ sattva-sanśhuddhir jñāna-yoga-vyavasthitiḥ
dānaṁ damaśh cha yajñaśh cha svādhyāyas tapa ārjavam

“Fearlessness, cleanness of life, steadfastness in the Yoga of wisdom, almsgiving, self-restraint and sacrifice and study of the Scriptures, austerity and straightforwardness, harmlessness, truth, absence of wrath, renunciation, peacefulness, absence of crookedness, compassion to living beings, uncovetousness, mildness, modesty, absence of fickleness, vigour, forgiveness, fortitude, purity, absence of envy and pride—these are his who is born with the divine properties, O Bharata.”

— Bhagavad Gita 16.1 · trans. Annie Besant (1922), public domain — via Wikisource

What this does NOT mean — these describe tendencies, not two kinds of people

The Gita here names divine and destructive qualities. It is not dividing humanity into permanently “good” people and “demonic” people. Every human heart holds both possibilities. The words describe tendencies we strengthen and choices we make — never fixed labels to pin on a person or a group. Reading this chapter as a licence to decide who is “divine” and who is “demoniac” is the exact misuse it warns against. The only heart we are asked to examine here is our own.

Words to Remember — abhayam fearlessness · sattva-sanśhuddhi purity of heart/mind · dāna charity, generosity · ārjava straightforwardness, integrity.

In plain words. These are not just admirable traits to collect. They are the natural expressions of a life guided by Dharma — a fearlessness that comes from having nothing to hide, and a straightforwardness that lets what we think, say, and do line up.

Reflect: Of these qualities, which one already feels most like you — and which would you most like to grow?

Verse 2 — The three gates · 16.21

त्रिविधं नरकस्येदं द्वारं नाशनमात्मन: ।
काम: क्रोधस्तथा लोभस्तस्मादेतत्त्रयं त्यजेत् ॥

tri-vidhaṁ narakasyedaṁ dvāraṁ nāśhanam ātmanaḥ
kāmaḥ krodhas tathā lobhas tasmād etat trayaṁ tyajet

“Triple is the gate of this hell, destructive of the self—lust, wrath, and greed; therefore let man renounce these three.”

— Bhagavad Gita 16.21 · trans. Annie Besant (1922), public domain — via Wikisource

Words to Remember — kāma selfish desire, craving · krodha anger · lobha greed · tyajet one should let go / abandon.

In plain words. Lord Shri Krishna names three forces that repeatedly pull people away from Dharma: desire driven by selfishness, anger that clouds judgment, and greed that is never satisfied. He does not shame us for feeling them — everyone does. He asks us to recognise them early, before they take root and quietly run the garden. Notice the verse says “abandon,” not “condemn.” The work is inner course-correction, not self-hatred.

Reflect: Which of the three — craving, anger, or greed — most often knocks at your gate, and what usually opens the door?

Lord Shri Krishna's purpose in this chapter is not to create fear or guilt. It is to awaken awareness. The Divine qualities bring inner freedom because they align our lives with Dharma; the opposite tendencies create suffering because they separate us from truth, wisdom, and compassion. Character is shaped not by one dramatic decision, but by the countless ordinary choices we make each day.

Dharma Lens

When honesty and loyalty pull in opposite directions

Chapter Sixteen praises truthfulness and compassion, straightforwardness and gentleness — which, in real life, do not always point the same way.

A situation without an obvious answer

Meera and her close friend both submitted the same class project. During grading, Meera realises the teacher mixed up their files and gave her friend the higher mark for work that was actually Meera's — while her friend, who is anxious about failing and whose family is going through a hard time, has said nothing. Correcting the error means telling the truth (integrity) and reclaiming what is fairly hers. Staying silent protects a struggling friend (compassion) but lets a falsehood stand and quietly teaches Meera that her own honesty is worth less than someone's comfort. Speaking up may cost the friendship; staying silent may cost something inside herself.

Is the Divine quality here truthfulness, or compassion? And can Meera honour one without betraying the other?

Chapter Sixteen does not hand Meera a script. It asks her to check her own heart before she acts: is she reaching for truth to correct a wrong, or partly to punish? Is she reaching for silence out of real kindness, or out of fear of an awkward conversation? A thoughtful person might resolve Meera's moment in more than one defensible way — quietly telling the teacher, speaking first to her friend, or finding a path that repairs the record without shaming anyone. What integrity rules out is the option that is only comfortable: pretending there is no choice to make at all.

Character in Practice · Integrity / Self-Correction

What it means. Living by the same values whether or not anyone is watching — and being willing to notice, admit, and correct your own mistakes.

What it does not mean. Being flawless, or loudly policing everyone else's faults. Integrity begins at home, in your own heart.

In daily life. Returning extra change; admitting “that was my fault” before you're caught; keeping a promise no one would have checked; correcting a rumour even when it flattered you.

How it's misread. As rigidity or self-righteousness. Real integrity is humble — it is quicker to correct itself than to accuse others.

Recognising progress — without self-righteousness. The gap between who you are in public and who you are in private slowly closes. Pair integrity with compassion, so that self-correction never curdles into self-punishment.

Student-Life Connection

The online test where the answers are one tab away and no proctor would ever know. The group chat where it would be so easy to pile on. The moment a cashier hands back too much change. The habit you keep telling yourself you'll quit “tomorrow.” Chapter Sixteen gently suggests: none of these are dramatic battles, and all of them are. Each is a small hand on the watering can, feeding one garden or the other.

Leadership Lab

Imagine you have been elected captain of your school's sports team. One teammate makes an important mistake during a game. You have two choices: embarrass them in front of everyone, or quietly encourage them, help them improve, and strengthen the team. Which response reflects true leadership?

Lord Shri Krishna teaches that leadership is not demonstrated through power. It is demonstrated through character. People willingly follow leaders whose actions inspire trust, respect, fairness, and compassion. The strongest leaders first learn to lead themselves.

Weekly Activity · The Gardener's Week

Purpose: to experience how a chosen quality strengthens through daily practice and honest review. Time: about 10 minutes to set up, then 5 minutes each night for a week (15–30 min total across the week) · Format: individual, with an optional class share-back · Materials: journal or notes app, a pen.

  1. Reread the Divine qualities in this chapter. Choose one to be your quality for the week — perhaps patience, truthfulness, compassion, humility, self-control, generosity, or forgiveness.
  2. Write it at the top of a fresh journal page, with one sentence on why you chose it.
  3. Each night, answer three short questions: When did I practise this quality today? What challenged me? What helped me succeed?
  4. Also notice its “weed” — the tendency that works against it (for patience, that might be irritation). Note one moment the weed showed up, without judging yourself.
  5. At week's end, write two or three sentences: What changed? Was character built through big moments, or small repeated ones?
  6. If your class shares back, offer only what you're comfortable sharing — some of this is private by design.
Try It This Week

The One Quality Practice

Great character grows through daily practice rather than occasional inspiration. Pick a single Divine quality and, each day, look for one real chance to choose it on purpose — then, at night, ask what challenged you and what helped.

You are not trying to be perfect. You are simply picking up the watering can each day and aiming it at the plant you want to grow.

Reflection & Journal Page

1. Personal. Read slowly through the Divine qualities. Which three do you already see growing within yourself?

2. Dharma. Which one quality would you most like to strengthen during the coming year, and why does it matter to you?

3. Character. Without judging yourself, name one habit or attitude that quietly weakens your character — and one practical step to loosen its grip this week.

4. Action commitment. The one Divine quality I will practise intentionally this week, and how I'll review it each night:

5. A private question (you never have to share this): If Lord Shri Krishna were sitting beside you today, which quality would He lovingly encourage you to cultivate, and which habit would He gently ask you to release?

A gentle note. This chapter asks you to look honestly at your own tendencies — and honest self-examination is not the same as self-attack. If reflecting here starts to feel like harsh self-judgment, pause. You are a garden being tended, not a case being prosecuted. Growth grows best in kindness. If difficult feelings linger, talk with a trusted adult, teacher, or counsellor — reaching out is itself a Divine quality.

Ask at Home

For a parent or guardian — please listen more than you speak. This is a conversation, not a quiz, and not a moment to test your child's honesty.

1. Is there a quality — like patience or fairness — that you've worked to strengthen in yourself over the years? What helped?

2. Can you remember a small, private choice from your own youth that you're still glad you made?

3. When you look at me, which quality do you see quietly growing that you'd want me to keep watering?

Teacher Note — Chapter 16

Objective: students can explain that character forms through repeated daily choices, name integrity and self-correction as the chapter's core virtue, and articulate the divine/destructive contrast as tendencies rather than fixed categories of people.

Sensitive-topic alert: the “divine vs. demoniac” framing is easily misread as sorting people (or groups) into good and evil. Hold firmly to the callout: these are tendencies within every heart, and the only heart the chapter asks us to examine is our own. Watch for any use of the text to label or exclude a classmate, family, or community.

Likely question: “So are some people just born demonic?” → No. Redirect to the garden image: everyone receives sun and rain; what differs is what we tend. The chapter is about direction of growth, not destiny.

Misconception to avoid: treating the verse list as a scorecard for judging others, or treating self-examination as self-condemnation. Distinguish honest self-correction from harsh self-blame.

Transition to Chapter 17: if the qualities we cultivate shape who we become, what shapes those qualities in the first place? Chapter 17 turns to faith — śhraddhā — and how what we trust quietly shapes what we become.
