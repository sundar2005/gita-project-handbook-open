Appendix A

Three Phases in Understanding the Gita

The eighteen chapters unfold in three natural phases — a journey from learning how to live, to knowing the Divine, to becoming who we are meant to be.

Phase OneChapters 1–6

Learning to Live

Scripture teaches us how to understand ourselves, our responsibilities, and the importance of disciplined action.

Phase TwoChapters 7–12

Learning to Know the Divine

Lord Shri Krishna reveals His Divine nature and teaches that devotion naturally transforms character.

Phase ThreeChapters 13–18

Learning to Become

Students discover their true identity, understand the forces that influence character, and freely choose to live according to Dharma.

Appendix B

The Eighteen Virtues

These virtues summarize the practical character lesson of each chapter, one for every week of the journey.

1Self-AwarenessWeek 1 · Arjuna Viṣhāda Yoga

What it isHonestly recognizing what is happening inside you — your feelings, motives, and fears — without pretending or blaming others.

What it is notEndless self-criticism, or obeying every emotion that arises.

Practise it byPausing to name a feeling (“I’m anxious,” “I’m angry”) before you act on it.

2WisdomWeek 2 · Sāṅkhya Yoga

What it isSeeing clearly what is temporary and what is lasting, and letting Dharma — not fear or pride — guide your choices.

What it is notBeing unemotional, or simply knowing many facts.

Practise it byBefore reacting, asking what is truly at stake and what is only passing.

3ResponsibilityWeek 3 · Karma Yoga

What it isDoing what is right because it is right, and owning the effect your actions have on others.

What it is notCarrying everyone’s burdens, or acting only for praise.

Practise it byCompleting one duty willingly and quietly, before anyone reminds you.

4HumilityWeek 4 · Jñāna Karma Sannyāsa Yoga

What it isBeing open to learning — willing to ask, to be corrected, and to admit you do not yet fully understand.

What it is notThinking poorly of yourself, or obeying blindly without understanding.

Practise it byAsking one sincere question each day, and truly listening to the answer.

5Inner PeaceWeek 5 · Karma Sannyāsa Yoga

What it isA steady heart that stays balanced through success and disappointment, while remaining fully engaged in life.

What it is notEscaping responsibility, or feeling nothing at all.

Practise it byDoing one everyday task with full attention, without rushing or complaining.

6Self-DisciplineWeek 6 · Dhyāna Yoga

What it isChoosing what is right over what is merely easy, and directing your attention instead of being driven by distraction.

What it is notPunishing yourself, or becoming rigid and joyless.

Practise it byKeeping one small, good daily habit for seven days — aiming for consistency, not perfection.

7WonderWeek 7 · Jñāna Vijñāna Yoga

What it isLooking at ordinary things with genuine appreciation, and sensing the Divine woven through life.

What it is notEmpty curiosity, or taking everything for granted.

Practise it byEach day, noticing one thing you would normally overlook, and pausing to appreciate it.

8SteadfastnessWeek 8 · Akṣhara Brahma Yoga

What it isStaying grounded in your values when life is uncertain — anchored, like a tree with deep roots.

What it is notRigidity, or refusing to grow and change.

Practise it byBeginning each morning by remembering one value you want to live by that day.

9DevotionWeek 9 · Rāja Vidyā Rāja Guhya Yoga

What it isA relationship with the Divine built on trust, gratitude, and love — expressed through how you live.

What it is notBlind belief, fear, or mere obligation.

Practise it byOffering one ordinary action — study, a chore, practice — sincerely, as a gift.

10GratitudeWeek 10 · Vibhūti Yoga

What it isNoticing the gifts already present in your life, and recognizing greatness without envy.

What it is notIgnoring real difficulties, or comparing yourself to others.

Practise it byWriting down three genuine things you are thankful for each evening — never repeating them.

11Humility through AweWeek 11 · Viśhwarūpa Darśhana Yoga

What it isThe humility that arises when we glimpse something far greater than ourselves, and our fears grow smaller.

What it is notFeeling worthless, or losing your confidence.

Practise it bySpending ten quiet minutes with something vast — the night sky, the ocean — simply observing.

12CompassionWeek 12 · Bhakti Yoga

What it isNoticing others’ struggles and responding with genuine kindness; strength that protects and serves.

What it is notWeakness, or pity that looks down on others.

Practise it byDoing one quiet act of kindness each day, without announcing it or expecting thanks.

13Self-Awareness (Deeper)Week 13 · Kṣetra Kṣetrajña Vibhāga Yoga

What it isObserving your thoughts and emotions without being controlled by them — knowing you are more than your roles.

What it is notDetaching from life, or ignoring how you feel.

Practise it byWhen a strong emotion rises, watching it for a moment before responding: “What am I experiencing, and why?”

14DiscernmentWeek 14 · Guṇa Traya Vibhāga Yoga

What it isRecognizing what helps you grow and what quietly pulls you away from your highest values.

What it is notHarsh judgment of others, or labeling people.

Practise it byNoticing which influences — people, habits, media — leave you clearer, and choosing more of those.

15PurposeWeek 15 · Puruṣhottama Yoga

What it isLiving rooted in what is highest and lasting, so your daily choices point in a direction that matters.

What it is notChasing status, or drifting wherever the moment pulls you.

Practise it byMaking one choice this week that reflects who you truly want to become, not just what is convenient.

16IntegrityWeek 16 · Daivāsura Sampad Vibhāga Yoga

What it isStrengthening the qualities that build good character, and honestly correcting the ones that harm it.

What it is notPretending to be perfect, or dividing people into “good” and “bad.”

Practise it byCatching one habit that does not reflect your values, and gently correcting it each day.

17AuthenticityWeek 17 · Śhraddhātraya Vibhāga Yoga

What it isActing from honest, sincere intention — being the same person inside as you appear outside.

What it is notPerforming for approval, or shaming others’ beliefs and practices.

Practise it byDoing one thing for its own sake — because you truly believe in it, not to be seen.

18CourageWeek 18 · Mokṣha Sannyāsa Yoga

What it isThe willingness to do what is right and act with clarity, even when it is hard or uncertain.

What it is notThe absence of fear, or acting recklessly without thought.

Practise it byMaking one clear, Dharma-guided choice you have been avoiding — and owning it.

Names & Relationships · Mahābhārata context review

Names & Relationships in the Mahābhārata

The Bhagavad Gita is a conversation set within the great epic, the Mahābhārata. The figures below are among the epic's central characters. Descriptions are kept general; students exploring the wider story should consult a reputable edition of the Mahābhārata. Mahābhārata context review

Lord Shri Krishna
The Divine teacher of the Gita, who speaks its wisdom to Arjuna and serves as his guide and charioteer.
Arjuna
The warrior-student to whom the Gita is spoken; one of the five Pāṇḍava brothers.
The Five Pāṇḍavas
Yudhiṣṭhira, Bhīma, Arjuna, Nakula, and Sahadeva — five brothers at the heart of the epic.
The Kauravas
The rival group of cousins, led by Duryodhana.
Dhṛitarāṣṭra
The blind king whose questions open the Gita's narration.
Sañjaya
The narrator who recounts the battlefield dialogue to Dhṛitarāṣṭra.
Bhīṣma
A revered elder of the family, honored across the epic.
Droṇa
A respected teacher of martial skill to the young princes.
Kuntī
Mother among the Pāṇḍava family.
Draupadī
A central woman of the epic, closely bound to the Pāṇḍava family.
Understanding Kurukshetra · Mahābhārata context review

Understanding Kurukshetra

Kurukshetra is remembered as a wide plain where two great armies gathered. The Bhagavad Gita takes place there in the moments before the fighting begins: Arjuna asks Krishna to place his chariot between the two sides, looks upon those he must face, and is overcome with doubt. The entire dialogue of the Gita unfolds in this pause — a conversation about Dharma that precedes the battle itself. In this book, "Kurukshetra" also stands for every situation in life where Dharma must be understood and lived. Mahābhārata context review

A Simplified Timeline · Mahābhārata context review

A Simplified Timeline

Kept intentionally general — a broad sketch of how the dialogue at Kurukshetra comes about within the epic. No specific dates are claimed. Mahābhārata context review

  1. A shared familyTwo groups of cousins — the Pāṇḍavas and the Kauravas — grow up within one royal family.
  2. A rift over duty and ruleA long dispute arises over what is right and who should rule.
  3. Efforts at peaceAttempts are made to resolve the conflict without war.
  4. The armies gatherWhen peace fails, the two sides assemble on the plain of Kurukshetra.
  5. The pause before battleArjuna, overcome with doubt, turns to Krishna — and the Bhagavad Gita begins.

Appendix F

Journaling Templates — My Gita Journal

The Bhagavad Gita is not a book to finish. It is a companion for life. Use these pages to continue your journey.

Reflect regularly on questions such as:

What challenged me this week?

How did I practice Dharma? Which virtue was easiest to live? Which was most difficult?

What did I learn about myself? Where did I experience gratitude? How did I serve someone else?

How did Lord Shri Krishna's teachings guide my decisions?

Date each journal entry. One day you will be able to look back and witness your own growth.

A Decision-Making Framework

"What Does Dharma Ask of Me Here?"

When a choice is hard, slow down and walk through these steps. The recurring question at the center is always the same: What does Dharma ask of me here?

1. Pause
Stop before reacting. Take a breath and give the decision the time it deserves.
2. Name feelings vs. facts
Separate what you are feeling from what is actually true. Both matter, but they are not the same.
3. Consider duties, intention, consequences
What are your responsibilities here? What is your honest intention? What are the likely effects on yourself and others?
4. Choose by Dharma
Ask the central question — What does Dharma ask of me here? — and let the answer guide the choice.
5. Act and reflect
Act with sincerity, then look back honestly. What did you learn for next time?
Discussion Guidelines

Ground Rules for Respectful Dialogue

These guidelines help every group session stay warm, honest, and safe.

  1. Listen to understand, not to reply. Let each person finish.
  2. Speak from your own experience; use "I" rather than "you."
  3. Disagree with ideas kindly — never with the person.
  4. Make room for quieter voices; share the air.
  5. Keep personal stories shared in the circle private.
  6. Ask sincere questions; it is fine to say "I don't know."
  7. Honor the text and one another with respect.
