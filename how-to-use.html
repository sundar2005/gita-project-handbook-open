How to Use This Book

The parts you will meet each week

Every chapter of this book follows the same gentle rhythm. Once you recognize the parts, you will always know where you are and what each moment is asking of you. Nothing here is a test. Each section is a different way of turning one timeless teaching toward your own life. Here is what you will meet, week after week.

Every life has moments where…
Each chapter opens with these words and a single feature line. They name an experience every human being eventually meets, and invite you to ask not only “What happened to Arjuna?” but “Where have I felt something similar?”
Today's Kurukshetra
A short retelling of where Arjuna stands in the conversation, and how his moment mirrors situations you may face — a difficult choice, a conflict between comfort and responsibility, a place where wisdom is required.
The Court of Dharma™
The six-step heart of every chapter: One Situation, One Dilemma, One Question, One Conversation, One Virtue, and One Life Practice. It is not a courtroom and no one is judged; it is a place of thoughtful inquiry where understanding grows.
Krishna's Teaching · the verses
A few verses from the chapter, each with the Sanskrit, a transliteration, a trusted translation, a short word list, and a plain-words explanation. Read these slowly. This is where you listen carefully beside Arjuna.
Dharma Lens
A real tension with no obvious answer, offered so you can weigh a situation honestly rather than reaching for a quick verdict. Often a thoughtful person could act in more than one way.
Character in Practice
A close look at the week's virtue — what it means, what it does not mean, how it appears in daily life, and how to recognize your own progress without self-righteousness.
Discussion Circle
Questions to explore together — observing, interpreting, connecting, weighing an ethical tension, and applying the teaching. The goal is honest conversation, not winning a debate.
The Weekly Activity
A hands-on experience — an interview, an observation, a small project — that lets the teaching move out of the page and into life before you talk about it again in class.
Try It This Week
One simple, practical practice to carry through your ordinary days. Wisdom becomes meaningful only when it is lived.
The Reflection & Journal Page
A set of prompts — personal, Dharma, character, an action commitment, and one private question you never have to share — for writing honestly in your own journal.
Ask at Home
A few questions to bring to a parent, guardian, or elder. These are conversations, not quizzes; the aim is to listen more than to speak.
The Teacher Note
Written for the adult guiding the group — the chapter's objective, any sensitive topic to frame with care, likely questions, and misconceptions to avoid. Students are welcome to read it too.
The Weekly Rhythm

Before, during, and after class

Each week has a natural shape. You will get the most from this book when you meet it in three unhurried movements rather than all at once.

Before class — Read the chapter opening and the verses slowly, on your own. Sit with the Every life has moments where… line and notice where it touches your week. Come with your curiosity, your questions, and even your doubts.

During class — Explore The Court of Dharma™ and the Discussion Circle together. Listen as much as you speak. Weigh the Dharma Lens tension honestly, and look closely at the week's virtue in Character in Practice.

After class — Live the teaching. Do the Weekly Activity and the Try It This Week practice, hold the Ask at Home conversation, and write on your Reflection & Journal page while it is still fresh.

Your Journal

A conversation with yourself

Learning becomes deeper when we take time to reflect. Throughout this curriculum you will keep a personal journal. This journal is not a test. It is not graded. It is a conversation with yourself.

At the end of every lesson you will be invited to write about your thoughts, questions, discoveries, and experiences. Some weeks your journal may contain only a few sentences. Other weeks you may fill several pages. Both are valuable. The goal is honesty, not perfection. Years from now, you may return to these pages and discover how much you have grown.

A Student Learning Covenant

A promise you make to yourself

A covenant is a promise made with care. Before you turn to Chapter One, read the words below slowly. If they describe the learner you wish to be, sign your name. This is not a promise to a teacher or a grade — it is a promise to yourself, to be returned to whenever the journey grows difficult.

As I begin this journey, I promise to learn with humility.

I will ask honest questions.

I will listen respectfully.

I will think deeply before forming conclusions.

I will encourage others to share their ideas with kindness.

I will seek to understand Dharma through the teachings of Lord Shri Krishna.

I will practice one virtue each week.

I will strive to become a person of integrity, compassion, courage, humility, and wisdom.

Most importantly, I will remember that learning is not about becoming better than others. It is about becoming the best version of myself.

Asking Difficult Questions

Guidance for respectful dialogue

Some students worry that asking questions shows weakness. The Bhagavad Gita teaches exactly the opposite. Arjuna asks many questions — some practical, some emotional, some philosophical — and Lord Shri Krishna welcomes every one of them. Questions asked with humility open the door to wisdom. Throughout this curriculum, never be afraid to ask Why? How? What does this mean? How does this apply to my life? Questions are not obstacles to learning. They are the pathway to understanding.

Some of the questions this book raises are difficult, and honest people will sometimes disagree. That is a good thing — but it asks something of us. A hard question is best explored in the spirit of a shared search for truth, not a contest to be won. These few guidelines help keep dialogue both fearless and kind.

Ask to understand — Let your question come from genuine seeking, not from a wish to trap someone or prove you are right. The most important question is often not the first one you ask.

Listen fully — Hear a person out before you respond. Try to understand their view well enough to state it fairly, even when you disagree with it.

Separate the idea from the person — You can question an idea firmly while treating the person who holds it with respect. Disagreement is not disrespect.

Make room for others — Encourage quieter voices to share, and welcome answers that surprise you. A circle grows wiser when more of it is heard.

Hold questions open — Some questions have no quick answer, and that is not a failure. It is an invitation to keep thinking, and to return as your understanding grows.

This curriculum supports reflection and character growth. Students carrying persistent worry or distress are encouraged to speak with a trusted adult and, when necessary, a qualified professional.
